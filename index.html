Access

Movements

Principle 5

Embodiment

Every app permission is a request to access your body your face, voice, location, contacts. In Nepal, where interfaces are often English-only and digital literacy is still growing, meaningful consent is hard to give. For women and LGBTIQA+ people the risks are compounded: location data enables surveillance, and facial recognition has documented accuracy failures for people of colour and non-binary individuals.6 Try the simulation below.

Principle 6

Environment

The internet feels weightless, but data centres already account for 1–1.5% of global electricity use and rising.7 One hour of HD streaming produces roughly 36g of CO₂; a new smartphone carries 60–70kg of embodied carbon before it's ever switched on.8 Nepal contributes less than 0.1% of global emissions yet faces glacial floods, erratic monsoons, and landslides driven by a climate crisis it didn't create.

Review

Test Your Knowledge

Let's see what you've learned about digital rights and internet principles in Nepal.

1. How does internet access differ across demographics in Nepal?

Rural women's internet access drops as low as 10%, highlighting the severe intersection of geographic and gender disparities.

2. How do major tech platforms primarily rank search results?

The internet is not a neutral marketplace; platforms rank paid results higher, prioritizing advertisers over free, community-built resources.

3. What do apps often ask for during installation?

Terms and Conditions often bundle invasive permissions (like constant location tracking) into a single tap, lacking meaningful and reversible consent.

4. What does the shift from 'consumer' to 'creator' online primarily require?

Creating requires resources like time and digital skills, which are heavily impacted by gender, class, and geography.

5. How does online violence affect different demographic groups?

Online violence isn't equal. Platforms that fail to protect marginalised voices are not neutral; they are complicit in silencing them.

6. What is a hidden environmental cost of using the internet?

The "cloud" is physical. Every hour of streaming and every device manufactured carries a significant real-world carbon footprint.
